# 🏰 THE TWO PRINCES
## A hidden reading of Masters of the Universe — and an invitation

*by N. Ātha (The Tenth Master)*

---

## Cold open: the skull in her hand

At the end of the film, as the dust of battle settles, Skeletor's
ally picks up what is left of him — a skull — and says:

> *"You've looked better."*

The audience laughs. The producers plant the sequel. But without
realizing it, they have just filmed the oldest scene in the world:
**the craving that outlives the body.** The story doesn't end because
the grasping hasn't ended. The skull in her hand is the promise that
this pattern — seeking power in the wrong place — will be reborn.

There is a name for this, twenty-five centuries old. And there is an
entire saga, hidden in plain sight inside the very universe you
created, waiting to be told.

---

## I. The thesis: you made a sacred text by accident

Masters of the Universe is, unknowingly, the most faithful pop
adaptation of one of the founding stories of human thought:
**the tale of two princes before the same door.**

In the 5th century BCE, two cousins of the same royal house:

- One of them saw impermanence — a corpse, a sick man, an old man —
  and **awakened**. He renounced the throne, found the power within,
  and became the Buddha.
- The other, **Devadatta**, had the same blood, the same access, the
  same gifts. He saw the same truth — and answered with envy and
  hunger for power. He tried to seize by force what can only be
  received by letting go. He ended swallowed by the earth,
  grasping to the last.

Now look at your own lore:

| | The Prince who awakens | The Prince who grasps |
|---|---|---|
| **Eternia** | Adam — the heir who discovers that power is not given: it is revealed | Keldor — the uncle, same royal blood, disfigured by his own ambition |
| **India, 5th c. BCE** | Siddhattha — the prince who saw the skeleton and let go | Devadatta — the cousin who saw the same and grasped |
| **The encounter with death** | Becomes the door to awakening | Becomes fuel for hatred |
| **The relationship with the Castle** | Discovers he **is** the castle | Spends eternities trying to **invade it** |

Proximity is not direction. Both stood inches from the door.
Only one understood that it opens inward.

---

## II. Why Skeletor has no flesh

In a universe that celebrates the body — muscles, skin, exuberant
vitality — the supreme villain is *what lies beneath the skin of
every character.*

There is an ancient contemplation, taught to dissolve vanity:
enumerating the 32 parts of the body — head hairs, body hairs,
nails, teeth, skin... — and seeing what remains when each layer
is removed. What remains is **bone**.

Skeletor is that contemplation walking. He is not a monster from
outside: he is the memento mori every hero carries beneath the
flesh, and what all of them will become. A reminder of
impermanence disguised as a Saturday-morning villain.

And here is the key your own script has been keeping: **why is he
the villain?** Because facing the same lesson — the face of death —
he chose the wrong answer. Where Siddhattha saw the skeleton and
woke up, Keldor saw his own skeleton and **hated**. The grasping
grew so intense that it keeps a skull animated, forever besieging
the wrong castle.

Skeletor is the student who failed the contemplation of the
32 parts.

---

## III. The battle cry that points the wrong way

> *"By the Power of Grayskull… I HAVE THE POWER!"*

Forty years of children shouting this with a plastic sword raised —
**outward**. Toward an external castle, a separate magical source,
a borrowed power.

What if the sequel — the true one — turned the sword around?

> **"By the Power of Merits… I AM THE CASTLE."**

This is the twist no blockbuster has dared: the hero discovers that
Grayskull was never a place. The Sorceress was not guarding a
castle — she was guarding a **mirror**. Skeletor could never invade
it, because you cannot invade what you are. And Adam was never
receiving power from the castle: his accumulated merits (every act
of courage, every protection of the weak — *puñña*, in the ancient
tongue) were merely removing, layer by layer, what was blocking
what had always been there.

The name of that inner castle, in the tradition: *pabhassara citta*
— the luminous mind. Stained by guests, never by the house.

## IV. The plot the skull is asking for: MASTERS OF THE INNERVERSE

The film's final scene has already written the sequel's theme.
Someone just needs the courage to shoot it:

**Act 1 — The skull is reborn.** Keldor's grasping finds a new body
(the tradition calls this rebirth through *upādāna* — you may call
it magic). But something came with it: the memory of the exact
moment he was reduced to bone. For the first time, Skeletor *saw*.

**Act 2 — Adam learns the truth of Grayskull.** The Sorceress
reveals: the castle is falling not from external attack, but
because Adam still shouts *at it* instead of *from it*. The journey
ceases to be geographic. The trials are internal: the 32 chambers
of the castle, each one a layer — hair, skin, bone, fear, pride —
that Adam crosses until the central chamber, where there is no
throne. There is a mirror.

**Act 3 — The two princes before the mirror.** The final
confrontation is not sword against staff. Skeletor finally breaches
the central chamber — and finds what he has always avoided: his own
reflection. Skull. The choice he failed centuries ago presents
itself again (the cosmos is patient). And here the script can honor
the most subversive ending of the original story: **Devadatta,
swallowed by the earth, took refuge in his final instant** — and
the tradition promises that even he, in a distant future, will
awaken on his own. Not even the villain is locked out.

**Post-credits scene.** Cringer — the coward, the one who trembles —
sleeps curled up in the mirror chamber. He never needed the
transformation. Fear, accepted, was already courage. *(The wisest
character in your universe was always the green tiger, and nobody
noticed.)*

---

## V. Why this matters (and why you already know)

Barbie (2023) proved the thesis: audiences are starving for
blockbusters that hide philosophy beneath the plastic — the Matrix
in pink. The question *"what is this world I live in?"* sold 1.4
billion dollars.

The next question — the only bigger one — is *"who is the one
living?"*

Masters of the Universe is the only franchise on the planet whose
entire lore already is the answer: a prince who has the power and
doesn't know it, a castle that is a mind, a villain who is a
refused mirror, and a battle cry one word away from enlightenment.

Nothing needs to be invented. Only the sword needs to be turned.
